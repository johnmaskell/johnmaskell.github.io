Visualising uncertainty in coastal flood predictions

Monday, 16th January 2017

Predicting coastal flooding at a particular return period has a degree of uncertainty associated with it. Some of the uncertainty may be safely ignored and will make little difference to the final simulated footprint. However, some of the uncertainty lies in such a range that it can make a significant difference to predicted flood footprint and can not be ignored when considering the possible consequences at a given return level. Some examples of uncertainty in a coastal flood modelling prediction for a given return period include:

  • The coastal surge level at the coast
  • The height of the defences
  • The DTM and the interpolation method if reinterpolated
  • The roughness coefficient possible derived from land use land cover data
  • The overtopping volume due to waves

The longer the return period the greater the uncertainty and the higher the consequences this uncertainty poses. Another challenge resides in relating this uncertainty in a meaningful way that is understandable to decision or policy makers that may have implications for coastal planning. If the uncertainty in the 100 year return period surge level is +/- 0.4 metres what does that really mean or look like for a given location?

This post aims to visualise the range of flood footprints due to the most significant sources of uncertainty. I have chosen to focus on Hornsea on the UK East Coast. Honsea has suffered from significant coastal erosion where sand transported by longshore drift (read more here) and subsequent cliff erosion has lead to a need to manage the regions natural defences to wave attack and flooding. Hornsea has also been flooded twice in recent times when storm surge and waves have overtopped the towns coastal defences. Significant flooding occured in December 2013 and more recently on January 17th 2017 where predicted storm surge levels called for an evacuation of part of the town as home and businesses suffered flooding. Hornsea is suseptable to coastal flooding for surge and wave levels that exceed the coastal defence elevation as it has a region of low lying land (seen here) where flood warnings are issued by the Envronent Agency. The environment agency also publish extreme sea levels in a coastal design sea levels database derived from extreme value analysis, joint probability and numerical modelling. The also publish a range of uncertainty based on the methods used to derive each return period.

In the following example I simulate the flood footprint (using Flood Modeller) at Hornsea due to the range of uncertainty in the 100 year return period coastal water elevation and the overtopping due to 3 m waves at the defences where the overtopping is controlled by the change in sea level that determines the freeboard. The range of uncertainty for the 100 year return return period water level at Hornsea is +/- 0.4 m. The range of uncertainty in the overtopping volme for the given wave condition can examined by subtracting or adding one standard deviation to the parameters in the equations that fits data and determines the mean overtopping discharge in the Eurotop manual. The manual recommends that you should increase overtopping discharge by one standard deviation from the mean for determing design heights. The default footprint is determined by the given 100 year return period water elevation and the mean overtopping topping discharge.
