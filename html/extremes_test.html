Extremes

JM Coastal can carry out extreme value analysis of observed and hindcast data. This can be combined with Monte Carlo simulations to create thousands of years of data and coastal flood simulations to provide in-depth flood risk analysis and input to coastal defence design.

The rarity of a coastal flood is often described by the return period (e.g. 1 in 100 years). Coastal defences are also often designed to prevent significant flooding for a certain return period that relates to the nearshore water level and wave conditions. However, different combinations of water levels and wave heights can combine to give the same return period but lead to different levels of coastal flooding. Therefore, to carry out a rigorous coastal flood risk assessment along a particular stretch of coast the joint probability of particular water levels occurring at the same time as certain wave heights should be investigated. Extreme water levels and wave height also usually exhibit a partial dependence (both are driven by the same storm) and therefore their correlation must also be taken into account.

Case study - Coastal flooding in New Brighton

Due to its location in the Eastern Irish Sea New Brighton is affected by a large tidal range with potential storm surges and large waves. An investigation of flood risk due to the combination of high water levels and wave was carried out using water level data from the tide gauge at Liverpool and the Liverpool bay WaveNet buoy. Returned periods for extreme sea level were determined using the Skew Surge Joint Probability Method (#ref). Generalised Pareto Distributions were fitted to a subset of the most extreme wave heights and skew surges using a maximum likelihood estimator to determine the extreme tails of the distributions of water levels and wave heights. A long term Monte-Carlo simulation (2000 years) that randomly samples the marginal and empirical distributions of water levels and wave heights taking into account their correlation was then carried out (#ref). The return period of the various combinations of water levels and wave heights could then be determined. Coastal flood simulations were then carried out for 8 samples of combined water levels and wave heights with a joint probability return period of 100 years.

An offshore model system is used to simulate the development of the tide and storm surge in the Irish Sea (Telemac2D). A high resolution nearshore modelling system (Telemac2D coupled with Tomawac) that couples the tide and surge with the wave is then used to simulate water levels and wave heights right up to the defence line. Only waves propagating from the west were simulated in this experiment which is the dominant wave direction in the Eastern Irish Sea as shown by the wave rose. At the defence line overflow and wave overtopping rates were calculated using equations detailed in the Eurotop manual (#ref) and used as boundary conditions to an inundation model (Flood Modeller) using a 5 m resolution grid aggregated from 1m lidar data from the EA open survey data.
